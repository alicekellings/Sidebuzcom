I watched my roommate make more money tutoring elementary school kids than I made with my CS degree.

That hurt.

Here I was, learning data structures and algorithms, spending 20 hours a week on problem sets, and my friend with a Communications major was out-earning me by helping third graders with multiplication tables.

Then I realized: I was thinking about this all wrong.

My coding skills weren't useless for side income. I was just applying them to the wrong things.

Once I figured out where to look, my income more than doubled. Now I make $1,500-2,000/month on the side while finishing my senior year.

Here's what I learned.

## Why CS Students Struggle to Monetize

There's a weird paradox with computer science students:

We have skills that companies pay $150k/year for. Yet most of us are broke until graduation.

Why?

Because we think the only way to use our skills is through a "real job" with a resume and interviews and all that. We forget that regular people - small business owners, content creators, researchers - need tech help RIGHT NOW and can't wait for you to graduate.

The opportunity is in that gap.

## Tier 1: Quick Money, Low Commitment

### 1. Fix Stuff for Local Businesses ($50-200/job)

Every small business has a website that's slightly broken. A form that doesn't work. A design that looks like 2008. An email that goes to spam.

You can fix that in an afternoon.

Walk into local shops. Find businesses on Google with terrible websites. Email them: "I noticed your contact form doesn't work. I can fix it for $75."

Most will ignore you. Some will pay. I made $400 my first month just fixing obvious problems.

### 2. Bug Bounties ($25-thousands)

HackerOne, Bugcrowd, and company bug bounty programs pay for finding security vulnerabilities.

The learning curve is steep, but if you're in a security class anyway, why not get paid to practice?

Fair warning: the easy bugs are gone. You're competing with professionals worldwide. But it's real money for real skills.

### 3. Technical Resume Reviews ($25-50/resume)

Other CS students are terrified of the job hunt. Your slightly more organized resume? Valuable.

Offer resume reviews to underclassmen. Include tips on which keywords parsers look for, how to describe projects, GitHub profile cleanup.

### 4. CS Tutoring ($30-60/hour)

Different from regular tutoring. CS tutoring commands a premium because the material is genuinely hard.

Intro to Programming students are desperate. Data Structures students are more desperate. Algorithms students will pay almost anything before finals.

Post on campus Facebook groups and in Discord servers for your classes.

## Tier 2: Freelancing (Real Income)

### 5. Web Development ($500-5000/project)

The obvious answer, but it actually works.

Start small. Friend's personal site. Club's event page. Local nonprofit. Build a portfolio of 3-5 projects, then start charging.

The money is in small businesses, not startups. Pizza shops need websites. Law firms need websites. They have money and low technical standards.

Pro tip: WordPress with a premium theme handles 80% of small business needs. You don't need to custom-code everything.

### 6. Data Analysis ($40-100/hour)

Can you use Python and Excel? Congratulations, you can do data analysis.

Startups, researchers, and small companies have messy data and no idea what to do with it. Clean their spreadsheets, make some charts, write a paragraph of insights.

If you're taking any statistics or ML classes, you're already qualified for basic data work.

### 7. Automation Scripts ($100-500/script)

People spend hours doing repetitive tasks. You can automate them.

Common requests:
- Scraping data from websites
- Formatting documents automatically
- Sending batch emails
- Generating reports from databases

One script = one payment. Doesn't scale infinitely, but $100 for a few hours of Python work is solid.

### 8. Discord Bot Development ($50-300/bot)

Gaming communities, crypto projects, and small businesses all want custom Discord bots.

Most requests are simple: verification systems, role assignment, basic moderation, custom commands.

If you know Python and can follow Discord.py documentation, you're qualified. The market exists on Fiverr and in Discord server listings.

## Tier 3: Product-Based Income

### 9. SaaS Microstartups

"Micro-SaaS" means tiny software products solving specific problems.

Examples that worked:
- A script that auto-generates social media images from blog posts
- A dashboard that tracks job application status
- A tool that schedules posts across platforms

Build something useful, charge $5-20/month, find 50-100 customers. Suddenly you have recurring income.

This takes more time upfront but compounds over time. Our [self-employed business idea finder](/self-employed-business) can help match you with specific opportunities based on your skills.

### 10. Templates and Boilerplates ($20-100 each)

Developers hate starting from scratch.

Create and sell:
- NextJS project boilerplates
- API templates
- Dashboard starter kits
- e-Commerce foundations

Gumroad is the easiest place to sell. One good template can earn passive income for years.

### 11. Course Creation (Long Game)

Know something other CS students struggle with? Make a course.

I'm not talking about $997 guru courses. I'm talking about $29 courses on Udemy or Skillshare about specific topics:
- "Deploy Your First App to AWS in 2 Hours"
- "Build a Portfolio Website with React"
- "API Basics for Non-CS Majors"

The production quality doesn't need to be Netflix-tier. It needs to be helpful.

### 12. Open Source + Sponsorships

Build something useful, make it open source, set up GitHub Sponsors.

This is a long game. Most open source developers make $0. But those who build genuinely useful tools? Some earn thousands monthly in sponsorships.

At minimum, it builds your reputation for future job searches.

## Tier 4: The Unexpected Options

### 13. Technical Writing ($50-200/article)

Developer blogs, documentation, tutorials - someone has to write them.

You don't need to be a writer. You need to be able to explain technical concepts clearly.

Companies like DigitalOcean, Twilio, and LogRocket pay $300-500 per technical tutorial. Medium's Partner Program pays less but has lower barriers.

### 14. Beta Testing for Startups ($50-200/month)

Startups need testers who can actually provide useful technical feedback.

Your bug reports are way more valuable than "it doesn't work." You can say "The API returns a 500 error when the request body exceeds 1MB."

Find startup communities, product hunt launches, and indie hacker groups. Offer your services.

### 15. Research Assistant Work ($15-25/hour)

Professors need help with technical research projects. Check your CS department's bulletin board or email professors directly.

It looks great on a resume, pays decent money, and might lead to actual research opportunities.

## What Actually Worked for Me

I'll be specific about my own income:

**Semester 5**: Made $0. Just doing coursework.

**Semester 6**: Started tutoring. Made $200/month.

**Semester 7**: Added web dev freelancing. Total: $600/month.

**Semester 8 (now)**: Tutoring ($400) + freelancing ($800) + one Micro-SaaS ($200/month in subscriptions) = ~$1,400/month.

That's 12-15 hours per week outside of classes. Manageable, but not casual.

## The Biggest Mistake I Made

I spent 4 months trying to build a "perfect" product before launching.

Should've launched in 4 weeks.

The first version sucked. But I learned what people actually wanted, iterated, and the current version is way better.

Perfection is procrastination. Ship something, get feedback, improve.

## How to Choose

Overwhelmed by options? Here's my framework:

**If you need money this week**: Tutoring or fix-it work for local businesses

**If you have a month**: Freelancing on Upwork/Fiverr

**If you're thinking 6+ months ahead**: Build a product or content

## The CS Advantage

Look, we're lucky.

While other majors need to learn technical skills from scratch, we already have them. The barrier to entry for us is lower.

The only thing we're missing is the mindset shift: YOUR SKILLS HAVE VALUE NOW, not just after graduation.

Start treating coding like a freelanceable skill. Because it absolutely is.
