Let me paint you a picture.

September 2023. I'm three weeks into college. My bank account has $127. Textbooks just cost me $340 I didn't have. And my meal plan is about to run out.

My options were: get a campus job (already missed the hiring window), ask my parents for money (absolutely not), or figure something else out.

I chose "figure something else out."

What followed was probably the most chaotic six months of random side hustle attempts you can imagine.

## The Stuff That Didn't Work

Before I tell you what finally clicked, let me save you some time with what flopped.

**Dropshipping**: I watched like 30 YouTube videos about this. Set up a Shopify store. Spent $150 on ads. Made exactly zero sales. The gurus conveniently forgot to mention you need real marketing skills and usually a few thousand dollars to actually make this work.

**Survey sites**: I spent six hours one weekend doing surveys on three different sites. Made $11.23. That's less than $2/hour. Never again.

**Selling stuff on campus**: This one wasn't terrible, but I ran out of stuff to sell after two weeks. Made about $200, but it's not sustainable unless you want to become a full-time reseller.

**"Influencer" attempts**: Started a TikTok about college life. Got maybe 200 views per video. Not exactly the brand deals I was imagining.

## The Turning Point

Around November, I had a conversation with an upperclassman in my stats class. She was working as an online tutor and making around $400/month for about 8 hours of work per week.

Wait, what?

She showed me the platform. The rates were decent. The schedule was totally flexible. And she was literally helping people with the same subjects she was already studying for.

That's when it clicked: **the best side hustle isn't whatever's trending on social media. It's whatever actually matches YOUR situation.**

She was good at stats and had a few hours between classes. Online tutoring made sense for HER.

For someone else - maybe a design major or someone who hates talking to people - tutoring would be miserable.

## What I Wish I'd Known Earlier

Here's what would've saved me months of fumbling:

**1. Know your actual constraints.** Not "I'll find the time" but literally how many hours you have. Write it down. Be honest. I had maybe 10 hours a week, max.

**2. Your major matters more than you think.** Not because you have to use it, but because you already have knowledge that others would pay for.

**3. Some side hustles need money to start.** Dropshipping, content creation, e-commerce - they often need upfront investment. If you're broke (like I was), that's not your starting point.

**4. The "perfect" option depends on your personality.** Some people love talking to strangers. Others want to work alone. There's no universal "best" - just "best for you."

## Finding What Actually Fits

After realizing all this, I got more strategic.

I asked myself:
- What do I already know that others struggle with?
- How much time do I really have?
- Do I want to interact with people or work solo?
- How fast do I need to make money?

For me, the answers pointed to freelance writing. I was an English major who could string sentences together. I could work at 2am if I wanted. And I didn't need any startup cash.

Started small on a content platform. First month: $180. Not amazing, but real money for work I could do in my dorm.

By month three, I was at $500/month. No dropshipping. No influencer stuff. Just writing articles about topics I understood.

## The Real Takeaway

Here's what I want you to walk away with:

The "best side hustle" lists online? They're written for nobody in particular. That's why they don't feel helpful.

What works for you depends on factors those articles never ask about. Your major. Your schedule. Whether you like people or prefer solo work. How much startup money you have (probably not much, if you're reading this).

Figure out YOUR constraints first. Then find something that actually fits them.
